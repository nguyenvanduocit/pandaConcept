# Bauhaus / Neo-Bauhaus Interior Design

---

## Philosophy
*Form follows function* elevated to ideology. Founded by Walter Gropius in Dessau, Germany, 1919. Rejected decorative art in favor of craft, industry, and mass production. The belief: good design should be accessible to all, achievable through industrial production. Color theory (Johannes Itten, Josef Albers), geometric reduction, and craft excellence define the aesthetic. The Bauhaus school operated 1919–1933 before being shut by the Nazi regime, leaving an influence that shaped virtually every strand of modern design.

---

## Core Characteristics
1. Geometric purity — squares, circles, triangles as the entire vocabulary
2. Primary colors (red, blue, yellow) against white-black-grey
3. Industrial materials celebrated: tubular steel, plate glass, concrete
4. No applied ornament — beauty only from proportion and material
5. Functional objects as art — chairs, lamps, pitchers designed as sculpture
6. Open floor plans before they were fashionable (1920s radical)
7. Flat roofs and white facades in architecture
8. Transparency and lightness through glass-steel construction
9. Grid-based layout discipline
10. Typography as graphic design element (Bauhaus lettering)
11. Workshop craft combined with machine production
12. Color theory: complementary contrasts create visual tension

---

## Materials
- **Tubular chrome steel** — Breuer Wassily chair, Mies Barcelona chair frames
- **Plate glass** — large windows, tabletops
- **Concrete (Béton brut)** — floors, feature walls
- **Bent/laminated plywood** — Aalto-influenced seating, shelving
- **Leather (black or tan)** — upholstery on steel-frame furniture
- **White-painted steel** — shelf systems, door frames
- **Lacquered MDF or Bakelite** — flat cabinet faces
- **Cane weaving** — Breuer-era seating inserts
- **Terrazzo** — flooring (original Bauhaus school had terrazzo throughout)
- **Rubber flooring** — black, industrial, workshop-origin
- **Enamel-coated steel** — kitchen and lab equipment aesthetic
- **Clear or frosted glass** — pendant shades, cabinet inserts

## Lighting
- Wagenfeld glass table lamp (original 1924 design — glass dome, opaline glass stem)
- Industrial metal pendants (enamel-coated hemisphere shades)
- Track lighting on exposed steel rails
- Floor-to-ceiling glazing as "natural light system"
- No decorative fixtures — functional, adjustable task lamps only

## Architectural Elements

- Polished concrete floors

## Neo-Bauhaus (2024–2025 Interpretation)
- Warm neutrals replacing clinical white (cream, linen, warm grey)
- Brass replacing chrome for hardware
- Soft curves entering Bauhaus geometry (arched doorways, rounded corners)
- Biophilic elements offsetting industrial harshness
- Color theory expanded beyond primaries (earth tones, deep jewels)
- Bauhaus typography and graphic art as large-format wall prints
- Terrazzo revival with warmer aggregate compositions

---

## Room Applications
- **Living:** White walls, Barcelona chairs, Noguchi-style coffee table, primary color rug, Wagenfeld lamp
- **Kitchen:** Flat-front white cabinetry, industrial steel shelving, terrazzo floor, enamel pendant
- **Bedroom:** Platform bed, minimal side table, one geometric artwork, no pattern anywhere
- **Bathroom:** White hexagonal tile, enamel fixtures, chrome accessories, grid mirror

- Neo-Bauhaus as a high-design institutional aesthetic for residential use
- Brass + Bauhaus — warm metal replacing chrome across all fixtures
- Graphic art wall prints referencing original school typography and geometry
- Biophilic counter-balance to industrial materials

## Bauhaus School History & Context

### The School (1919–1933)
- Founded by Walter Gropius in Weimar, moved to Dessau (1925), then Berlin (1932)
- Shut by Nazi regime 1933; faculty dispersed globally — Moholy-Nagy to Chicago, Albers to Yale, Mies to Illinois Institute of Technology
- Three directors: Walter Gropius (1919–1928), Hannes Meyer (1928–1930), Mies van der Rohe (1930–1933)
- Three locations: Weimar → Dessau → Berlin

### Key Workshops
- **Weaving Workshop** (Gunta Stölzl): geometric tapestries, wall hangings, textile theory
- **Metal Workshop** (László Moholy-Nagy): light and space experiments, Wagenfeld lamp
- **Furniture Workshop** (Marcel Breuer): tubular steel revolution
- **Typography** (Herbert Bayer): universal typeface, grid discipline
- **Preliminary Course** (Johannes Itten, then Moholy-Nagy): color theory foundation

### Color Theory (Itten/Albers)
- Seven color contrasts: hue, light-dark, cold-warm, complementary, simultaneous, saturation, extension
- Complementary colors vibrate against each other (red/green, blue/orange, yellow/violet)
- Bauhaus interiors deploy this deliberately: red wall against green plant; yellow lamp against purple chair

## Neo-Bauhaus Room by Room (2024–2025)

### Living Room
- White or warm cream walls; one primary color accent (red rug or blue cushion)
- Barcelona or Wassily chairs on polished concrete or terrazzo
- Wagenfeld-style table lamp; industrial track on ceiling
- One large geometric artwork (Bauhaus poster reproduction or original)
- No decorative objects — every object is functional

### Kitchen
- Flat-front cabinetry in white or warm grey; no hardware or push-to-open
- Industrial steel open shelving for display
- Terrazzo floor (white base, grey aggregate)
- Enamel-coated pendant over island
- Rubber or cork flooring in utility zone

### Bedroom
- Platform bed with no headboard or geometric slab headboard
- One geometric artwork — Klee, Kandinsky, or Bauhaus typography print
- Minimal side table (tubular steel frame, glass or leather top)
- No pattern anywhere; texture from material alone (linen, leather, wool)

### Bathroom
- White hexagonal tile floor, white subway wall tile
- Chrome (traditional) or unlacquered brass (Neo-Bauhaus) fixtures
- Grid mirror with industrial frame
- Enamel basin on steel frame
