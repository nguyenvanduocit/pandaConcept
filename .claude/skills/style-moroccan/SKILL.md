# Moroccan / Moorish Interior Design
## Riad / Medina / Berber-Andalusian Tradition

---

## Historical Context

Moroccan interior design is the product of an extraordinary 1,300-year synthesis of Berber indigenous culture, Arab-Islamic civilization, Andalusian refinement (the expelled Muslims and Jews of Spain after 1492 brought their sophisticated Nasrid aesthetic to Morocco), Ottoman influence, and brief French/Spanish colonial presence.

The Moorish style reached its artistic apex in three periods: the **Almohad dynasty (12th–13th century)**, the **Marinid dynasty (13th–15th century**, builders of Fez's great medersas including the Bou Inania Medersa), and the **Saadian/Alaouite dynasties (16th century onward)**. The architecture of the Alhambra in Granada and the Bou Inania Medersa in Fez represent the pinnacle of the tradition.

**Riad** — the defining Moroccan domestic typology; inward-facing house organized around a central courtyard with fountain; from Arabic rawdah (روضة, garden); rooms open to courtyard, not to street; privacy paramount. The riad conceals all beauty behind plain exterior walls — a fortress outside, a paradise garden within.

**Medina architecture** — the labyrinthine medinas of Fez (oldest), Marrakech, and Meknes; exterior walls blank and fortress-like; all ornament turned inward; the ornate street door (bab) is the only outward signal of interior richness.

**Islamic art principles** — aniconism (theological prohibition on depicting living beings) led Islamic artists to unparalleled mastery of three decorative languages: geometric pattern (infinite tessellations reflecting divine order), calligraphy (Quranic verse as ornament), and arabesque (vegetal scroll, infinite and self-renewing).

**Philosophical foundations:** Islamic theology — geometric pattern reflects divine order; infinite repetition = divine infinity; Andalusian refinement — poetry, music, philosophy as design context from the expelled Nasrid civilization; Berber craft tradition — woven textiles, kilim rugs, pottery as the ancient indigenous base; Water symbolism — fountain at center of riad = Paradise garden (Quran: "gardens beneath which rivers flow").

## Core Characteristics

1. Zellige tilework (زليج) — hand-cut fired clay mosaic in complex geometric patterns; lower walls; Fez production center
2. Carved stucco (gebs جبس) — white plaster carved into arabesque and geometric relief; middle wall band
3. Carved cedar (zouaq) — painted and carved Atlas cedarwood; upper walls and coffered ceilings
4. Central courtyard fountain (shadirwan) — riad's defining feature; cooling through evaporation; meditative sound of water
5. Muqarnas (مقرنصات) — three-dimensional stalactite/honeycomb vaulting; transitions between wall and ceiling
6. Tadelakt plaster (تادلاكت) — smooth lime plaster; waterproof through black soap polishing; traditional hammam finish; silky surface
7. Islamic geometric patterns — infinite repeat tessellations based on star polygons (6, 8, 10, 12-point stars)
8. Arabic calligraphy — Quranic verses as decorative frieze band in carved stucco or tilework
9. Colored glass lanterns (fanous فانوس) — pierced metal with colored glass; hanging; cast star-polygon shadow patterns
10. Beni Ourain rugs (بني وراين) — undyed ivory wool with black geometric motifs; Atlas Berber tribe; globally iconic
11. Leather poufs (hassock) — hand-stitched camel or goat leather; floor seating clusters in 3–5 pieces
12. Mashrabiya screens (مشربية) — carved wood lattice projecting bay window; privacy and ventilation simultaneously
13. Low banquette seating (banquette marocaine) — built-in perimeter bench with bolster cushions; lines room edges
14. Hammered brass and copper — lanterns, tea trays (siniya), tea sets; warm metallic accent throughout

## Furniture

**Banquette marocaine (built-in seating)** — L-shaped or perimeter bench built into the room; timber or masonry base; cushioned with fabric-covered foam; bolster cushions stacked at ends and back; the primary seating form replacing individual chairs entirely; defines the Moroccan salon.

**Poufs (hassock)** — hand-stitched leather; floor seating; clusters of 3–5 in various sizes; traditional colors: camel yellow, cobalt blue, emerald green, ox-blood red; the single most exported Moroccan design object globally.

**Carved cedar sideboard (commode)** — Fassi (Fez) tradition; geometric bone inlay on drawer fronts; hammered brass hardware; heavy Atlas cedar construction; dark finish.

**Siniya (tea tray table)** — large round hammered brass tray (often 60–80 cm diameter) on folding cedar base; used for the ceremonial tea service and as coffee table; the center of Moroccan hospitality.

**Moucharabieh screen (mashrabiya)** — freestanding or built-in latticed wood screen; double function of privacy and air circulation; geometric pattern based on circle-and-polygon construction; can be single panel or full room divider.

**Mosaic table (table zellige)** — round or octagonal table; zellige mosaic top in complex geometric pattern; wrought iron base; indoor/outdoor use; iconic Moroccan piece found globally.

## Lighting

- **Fanous lanterns (فانوس)** — pierced brass or iron; stained glass inserts in star shapes; hanging from ceiling in clusters; project star-polygon shadow patterns onto surrounding surfaces — the defining Moroccan lighting signature that transforms any room
- **Berber candles** — large altar candles in brass holders; warm ambient light; essential for evening atmosphere
- **String lights through mashrabiya** — modern addition; warm LED threaded through carved wood screen creates complex moving shadow when air moves
- **Hammam diffused light** — star-shaped ceiling apertures (shamsiyat) cast dramatic light shafts; traditional hammam light quality transferred to bathrooms
- **Courtyard midday zenith** — central riad courtyard designed to receive direct midday sun onto fountain; surrounding rooms remain cool and shaded; the thermal engine of the riad
- **Modern** — brass pendant lanterns with warm Edison bulbs; colored glass for deep-toned ambient rooms; 2200–2700K throughout; star shadow projection as non-negotiable feature

## Architectural Elements

**Riyad (central courtyard)** — the soul of the house; paved in zellige mosaic; central fountain (shadirwan شادروان); orange or lemon trees in corners; all room openings face inward; sky visible above; air conditioning through thermal convection; the Paradise garden made architectural.

**Iwan** — vaulted open-fronted reception niche; arched opening; elaborately decorated interior with all three decorative layers; seat of honor placed inside; the formal receiving space.

**Bab (decorated door)** — the street door is the only facade ornament; Atlas cedar; carved geometric and arabesque; hammered brass knockers (often Khamsa form); proportions follow Islamic geometric principles.

**Three-layer wall system:**
1. Bottom: zellige wainscoting (100–150 cm height)
2. Middle: carved stucco arabesque band (to 250 cm height)
3. Top: painted/carved cedar frieze and coffered ceiling

**Arched openings** — horseshoe arch (Moorish); multifoil arch; pointed arch; always ornamented at keystone with muqarnas cluster or calligraphic band.

**Hammam (traditional bath)** — sequence of cold (bayt al-barid), warm (bayt al-wastani), and hot (bayt al-harara) rooms; tadelakt surfaces throughout; star ceiling apertures (shamsiyat); steam; integral to traditional Moroccan house or neighborhood.

## Decorative Arts

- **Zellige mosaic art** — Fez mallem (master craftsmen); complex 8-point star (khatam) patterns as foundation; 6-point, 10-point, and 12-point stars also standard; centuries-old geometric traditions transmitted by apprenticeship; each tile hand-cut
- **Fassi pottery (Fez blue)** — cobalt blue painted on cream ground under clear glaze; Fez city tradition for 1,000+ years; floral arabesque and geometric patterns; kiln workshops still active in the medina
- **Safi pottery** — bright multicolor; fish and geometric motifs; more rustic and graphic than refined Fez blue; Atlantic coastal city tradition
- **Khamsa (Hand of Fatima / خمسة)** — five-fingered protective amulet; used as door knocker, wall plaque, and jewelry; both Islamic and Berber traditions claim it; protection against the evil eye
- **Berber jewelry** — silver with amber, coral, and enamel; Amazigh (Berber) tribal origin; displayed in frames or on surfaces as art; fibula (tizerzai) brooches
- **Calligraphy panels** — Quranic verses in Kufic or Thuluth script; framed and hung; sacred text as visual art

## Room-Specific Applications

**Entry (driba درب)** — right-angle bent entry corridor preventing direct view into courtyard from street; zellige floor; ornate door; transition from street chaos to interior calm; the spatial decompression sequence.

**Salon (reception room)** — banquette perimeter seating lining all walls; siniya tea table at center; zellige or tile floor; fanous pendant lanterns; carved stucco walls; Beni Ourain rug as floor anchor; the complete Moroccan room.

**Bedroom** — low bed with carved cedar headboard; Moroccan velvet cushions stacked; embroidered pillows; tadelakt accent wall; fanous bedside lamp; rich textile layers on bed; intimate and enclosed.

**Courtyard** — zellige fountain at center; terracotta pots with lemon and orange trees in corners; low banquette along wall; candles in the evening; the heart of the riad experience.

**Hammam (bathroom)** — tadelakt surfaces on all walls and floor; round hammered brass basin; star-aperture ceiling light shaft; warm steam; bench seating in wet area; richly atmospheric.

## Modern Interpretations (2024–2025)

- **Riad boutique hotel** — the global luxury hospitality benchmark: antique tiles, maximalist textiles, candlelight, and courtyard fountain; the Marrakech riad hotel defined contemporary luxury travel
- **Moroccan minimalism** — single zellige-tiled accent wall; one Beni Ourain rug; one fanous lantern; restraint as a counterpoint to the tradition's maximalism
- **Monochrome zellige** — entire bathroom or kitchen in single color zellige (all white, all terracotta, all cobalt blue); color saturation through material density
- **Global boho** — Moroccan pouf + kilim rug + brass lantern as the foundation of Western eclectic interiors; Moroccan as the international bohemian baseline
- **Tadelakt surfaces everywhere** — bathroom, kitchen backsplash, and bedroom feature wall in contemporary homes worldwide; waterproof plaster as luxury material
- **Dark Moroccan** — midnight blue or black zellige; brass lanterns at low intensity; maximalist cushions; deep candlelit atmosphere

## Cultural Sensitivity Notes

- Quranic calligraphy as decorative element requires respect — do not distort, rotate, or use as mere pattern; the text carries sacred meaning
- Khamsa (Hand of Fatima خمسة) is a religious protective symbol, not mere decoration; render as a meaningful protective amulet
- Aniconism in Islamic art means no figurative painting on walls — ornament is abstract/geometric/calligraphic by theological principle; do not add human or animal figures to traditional wall treatments
- Distinguish Moroccan from generic "Middle Eastern" — Morocco's Berber-Andalusian tradition is unique and distinct from Arab Gulf, Turkish, or Iranian aesthetics
- Traditional Moroccan spaces are gender-divided: public salon (open to guests) vs. private family spaces; this spatial organization is culturally meaningful

## Complementary Styles

- **Persian** — shares Islamic geometry, muqarnas, calligraphic tradition, and tile work; differs in color palette (Persian uses more turquoise/lapis) and the central importance of the Persian carpet
- **Turkish/Ottoman** — related through Islamic design vocabulary; Moroccan is more Andalusian and Berber; Ottoman is more symmetrical and imperial in scale
- **Bohemian** — global bohemian style has absorbed Moroccan elements (poufs, kilim rugs, brass lanterns) as its primary exotic vocabulary; a natural pairing
- **Indian Mughal** — shares Islamic geometric vocabulary and luxury craft materials; both traditions descended from the same Persian source
