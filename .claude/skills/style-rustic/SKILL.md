# Rustic Interior Design

---

## Philosophy
Authenticity over polish. The beauty of materials in their natural, unprocessed state — rough timber, raw stone, split logs, hand-forged iron. Rustic celebrates age, weathering, and the evidence of human hands. It is the antithesis of mass production and synthetic surface. Three distinct regional traditions — American Western Lodge, European Farmhouse (Provence/Tuscany/Alpine), and Mountain Lodge — share the same core conviction: that beauty is inherent in honest materials.

---

## Core Characteristics
1. Exposed log or heavy timber framing — structural and visible
2. Stone fireplace as room anchor (often floor-to-ceiling)
3. Rough-sawn or hand-planed lumber for floors and walls
4. Reclaimed materials with visible history — nail holes, saw marks, patina
5. Animal hide and fur textiles — cowhide, bear skin, sheepskin
6. Wrought or hand-forged iron hardware and light fixtures
7. Plank-and-beam ceilings — visible structural joinery
8. Natural stone floors — flagstone, slate, river rock
9. Antler chandeliers or shed-antler accent pieces (shed antler — ethical)
10. Taxidermy or wildlife-inspired art (American lodge tradition)
11. Hand-woven rugs — Navajo (American), Kilim (European)
12. Abundant wood grain variation — knots, checks, figure celebrated not concealed

---

## Regional Variations

### American Rustic (Western Lodge)
Log construction, frontier heritage, Native American craft, Rocky Mountain setting. Large-scale rooms with trophy room tradition. Cowhide, branded leather, antler chandeliers. Navajo blankets and Pendleton textiles. Cedar and Douglas fir logs.

### European Rustic (Provence, Tuscany, Alpine)
Farmhouse heritage, stacked stone, plaster and timber, pastoral refinement. French lavender and Provençal tile. Tuscan olive press converted to home. Alpine chalet with heavy fir beams, embroidered linens, ceramic stove (*Kachelofen*).

### Mountain Lodge
Grand scale, ski chalet luxury, lakeside cabin grandeur. Plunge pools, stone soaking baths, double-height great rooms. Higher material quality — cashmere throws, mohair upholstery, artisan ceramics — while retaining structural rawness.

## Architectural Elements
- Exposed log or post-and-beam framing throughout
- Stone chimney breast running full height of room
- Covered porch or wraparound verandah
- Cathedral ceiling with exposed ridge beam
- Wide-plank flooring (planks 15cm+ wide) with visible saw marks
- Stone threshold connecting exterior to interior

- **Living:** Stone fireplace, exposed beam ceiling, leather sofa, cowhide rug, antler chandelier, Navajo textile

## Log Construction Deep Dive

### Wood Species by Region
- **Douglas Fir** — Pacific Northwest/Rocky Mountain; straight, strong, golden-orange; most common American log cabin material
- **Western Red Cedar** — Pacific Coast; aromatic, rot-resistant; ideal for exterior log walls
- **White Pine** — New England/Appalachian; softer, knotty; traditional eastern cabin
- **Chestnut** — European farmhouse; now rare due to blight; reclaimed pieces highly prized
- **Oak (Quercus)** — European lodge and alpine; dense, dark grain; heavy timber framing

### Construction Methods
- **Full-scribe log** — each log hand-fitted to the one below; no visible gaps; most traditional
- **Chinked log** — logs with intentional gap filled with mortar or chinking compound; visual texture
- **Post-and-beam** — structural posts at corners with timber beams; allows large windows between posts
- **Timber frame** — mortise-and-tenon joinery; pegs visible; European farmhouse tradition

---

## Stone Fireplace Construction
The stone fireplace is the non-negotiable anchor of every rustic interior. Key considerations:

- **Stone types:** Fieldstone (rounded river stones), flagstone (flat slabs), granite (quarried), limestone, river rock
- **Mortar color:** Grey or buff — never white (looks suburban)
- **Dimensions:** Floor-to-ceiling preferred; minimum 2m wide; hearth extends 50cm+ into room
- **Mantel:** Solid timber beam (rough-hewn, single piece); minimum 15cm thick
- **Firebox:** Standard wood-burning or insert; gas acceptable but visual authenticity of wood preferred

## Navajo & Native American Textile Reference

### Navajo Weaving Traditions
- **Two Grey Hills** — brown, black, white, cream only; geometric diamond and stepped patterns; Shiprock, New Mexico
- **Ganado** — red, black, white, grey; bold geometric; named for Hubbell Trading Post town
- **Crystal** — flowing patterns, vegetal dyes; softer than geometric styles
- **Yei** — figures of Navajo Holy People; sacred narrative textiles

### Ethical Sourcing
- Purchase directly from Navajo Nation weavers or certified Navajo-owned galleries
- Avoid mass-produced imitations marketed as "Southwestern" or "Navajo-inspired"
- Certificate of authenticity from Navajo Nation or Indian Arts and Crafts Board recommended

## European Rustic Sub-Styles

### Provençal (Southern France)
- Lavender field palette: lavender, sunflower yellow, sage green, terracotta
- Toile de Jouy fabric (scenic pastoral print on cream)
- Faience tile (Moustiers-Sainte-Marie style)
- Wrought iron furniture and hanging pot rack
- Provençal farmhouse (*mas*): low-pitched roof, thick stone walls, blue-painted shutters

### Tuscan Farmhouse (Italy)
- Rough chestnut ceiling beams, terracotta floor, plaster walls
- Dark walnut furniture, iron hardware
- Olive and vine references throughout
- See Italian Mediterranean file for full detail

### Swiss/Austrian Alpine
- Heavy carved pine furniture (Tyrolean style)
- Ceramic tile stove (*Kachelofen*) as room anchor
- Embroidered linen and cross-stitch textiles
- Antler mounts and hunting trophy tradition
- Deep eaves, balconies with carved railings
